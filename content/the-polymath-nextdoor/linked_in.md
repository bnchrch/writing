Everyone is missing the non-technical engineer.

I rarely post on LinkedIn. But after a string of conversations over the last couple of years, I think leaders in established industries are missing what LLMs have done to professionals, what the new user persona looks like, and what that means for their products.

AI didn't just make existing engineers faster. It collapsed the beginner-to-intermediate curve in software so completely that people from unrelated fields are building real systems, shipping them, and developing the exact same frustrations that traditional engineers have.

I keep running into these people. I've started calling them **non-technical engineers.** The "non-technical" part describes their history, not their ability. **The barrier was the tooling, not the talent.**

Four I've actually met, because the variety is the point:

A former CFO who runs three veterinary clinics vibe-coded two polished mobile apps for radical financial transparency. Employees see salaries, revenue targets, and submit improvement ideas, all gamified in a Stardew Valley theme. More polished than some of my MVPs after 15 years of building software.

My neighbor is a dentist in a small town in Canada. He engineered his own pipeline for same-day crowns: an array of 3D printers, calibration cubes for accuracy, integrations between multiple software packages. Scan, print, fit, same appointment.

At Airbyte, I helped build a system that let people publish, fork, and remix API integrations. We bolted on an AI assistant to help users write them. Internally, there was real debate: "What's the lowest barrier of technical ability we're willing to support?" Engineers were skeptical that non-technical users would actually show up in this part of the product. They were wrong. A chef who owned a restaurant chain built integrations to pull data from his POS system. Sales reps authored connectors for obscure internal tools. People who had never written code were building and publishing integrations that other customers used. I'm seeing this play out in other ways now too.

A CEO and former CMO showed me his "content operating system." Custom scripts, a GitHub repo, markdown files, and AI agents that produce content in his voice. Without knowing it, he'd re-derived data engineering best practices: idempotent transformations, staged processing, version-controlled configs.

None of these people call themselves engineers. But when they hit the limits of the software they use, they sound exactly like us:

"Why can't I access my own data through an API?"
"If I can see it in the UI, why isn't it in the API?"
"Where's the webhook?"

The vet practice owner is now switching patient management vendors. Not because the software is bad at managing patients. Because it's bad as a platform.

In a small town in Canada and a modest professional network, I found a CFO building mobile apps, a dentist running a 3D printing pipeline, a chef writing data integrations, and a marketer building a text-processing pipeline in markdown.

**If I can find this many in a small radius, they're everywhere.**

Your product isn't software they use. It's a platform they build on. And they're going to want an API key.
