# Writing Well

Universal writing principles. These apply regardless of personal voice or content type.

## Foundations

**Clarity is the goal.** Clear thinking produces clear writing. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.

**Every word must earn its place.** After drafting, ask: can I cut 20% without losing meaning? You almost always can.

**Respect the reader's attention.** Their time is valuable. Front-load the most important information. Don't make them wade through preamble to find the point.

## Principles

### Be Concrete
Abstract language hides weak thinking. Use specific examples, real numbers, actual names. "We improved performance" tells the reader nothing. "Response time dropped from 800ms to 120ms" tells them everything.

### Use Active Voice
Active voice is direct, clear, and shorter. "The function processes the request" beats "The request is processed by the function." Passive voice has its place (when the actor is unknown or unimportant) but default to active.

### Show, Don't Declare
Don't tell the reader something is "interesting" or "important." Show them why through evidence, examples, or the implications. Let them draw their own conclusions.

### One Idea Per Paragraph
Each paragraph should make one point. If you find yourself connecting two different ideas with "Also" or "Additionally," split them.

### Vary Sentence Length
Monotonous rhythm loses readers. Mix short, punchy sentences with longer, flowing ones. Short sentences land key points. Longer ones build nuance and complexity. This creates a natural reading rhythm that keeps attention.

### Choose Strong Verbs
Weak verbs need adverbs as crutches. "Ran quickly" becomes "sprinted." "Said loudly" becomes "shouted." The verb should do the heavy lifting.

### Read It Aloud
If it sounds awkward spoken, it reads awkward too. Reading aloud catches clunky rhythm, unnecessary words, and unclear phrasing that your eyes skip over.

## The Editing Test

Before publishing, ask:
1. Does it sound like a human wrote it?
2. Would you actually read this if someone else wrote it?
3. Can you cut 20% more?
4. Is there a simpler way to say this?
5. Does the opening earn the reader's attention in the first two sentences?
